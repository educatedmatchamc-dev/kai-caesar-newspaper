Breaking News

CAESAR IS DEAD: STABBED IN THE SENATE

Twenty Three Wounds End the Life of Rome’s Greatest Leader — Brutus Among the Killers — The City Is in Chaos

By Gaius Flavius Maximus · Senior Correspondent, The Roman Tribune

It is a dark, terrible day for Rome. Julius Caesar, the Dictator of Rome and probably the greatest general this city has ever seen, was stabbed to death yesterday morning inside the Theatre of Pompey during a regular meeting of the Senate. He was fifty five years old. He went in just like any other morning and he never came back out. The men who killed him are calling themselves the “Liberators.” Most Romans we talked to today are calling them something a lot less polite.

A large group of senators surrounded Caesar at the base of a statue of Pompey and stabbed him twenty three times. Doctors who looked at the body later said that only one of those wounds was actually deadly enough to kill a man. The other twenty two were like angry bees stinging a lion, lots of pain but not enough on their own to take down the king of beasts. Simile

The whole thing was planned by Gaius Cassius Longinus, a senator who had been jealous of Caesar for a very long time. He spent weeks convincing other senators to join him, telling them that killing Caesar was the only way to save the Roman Republic from turning into a kingdom with one man ruling over everything forever.

But the part of this story that has Romans actually crying in the streets, the part that nobody can stop talking about, is this: one of the men holding a knife was Marcus Junius Brutus. Caesar’s best friend. A man Caesar had personally protected after a battle years ago. A man Caesar trusted more than almost anyone else in the world. When Caesar looked up from the ground and saw Brutus standing over him with a bloody blade, he stopped trying to fight back. He pulled his toga over his face, said his last three words — “Et tu, Brute?” meaning “Even you, Brutus?” — and then he was gone.

Caesar had been warned. Weeks ago a soothsayer stopped him in the street and said “Beware the Ides of March.” Caesar just laughed. Then yesterday morning, on his way into the Senate, he passed that same soothsayer again and said “Well, the Ides of March are come.” The soothsayer looked at him and said very quietly, “Ay, but not gone.” A few hours later Caesar was dead. Irony — he was handed the warning twice and ignored it both times.

Mark Antony standing over Caesar’s body as he addresses thousands of Romans in the Forum. The crowd’s grief quickly turned to rage.

His wife Calpurnia had begged him not to go that morning. She told him she had nightmares about his statue pouring blood like a fountain while smiling Romans washed their hands in it. She was completely right. Caesar should have listened. But his confidence was as tall as the Colosseum and just as hard to knock down, and he walked out the door anyway. Hyperbole and Metaphor

Mark Antony, Caesar’s closest ally and co-consul, was not allowed inside the Senate chamber during the attack. A conspirator named Trebonius kept him busy outside on purpose. Many people believe that if Antony had been in that room, the whole thing might have ended very differently.

After the killing the conspirators walked out onto the steps of the Capitol and shouted that Rome was now free. They expected cheering. What they got was silence and then fear. By evening the streets were full of angry citizens. Rome does not feel free tonight. Rome feels broken.

Rome · The Forum

ANTONY TURNS THE WHOLE CITY AGAINST THE CONSPIRATORS WITH ONE SPEECH

By Lucia Cornelia Tiberia · Forum Correspondent

I have covered many speeches in the Roman Forum over the years. I have never seen anything like what Mark Antony did yesterday afternoon. What started as a funeral turned into a storm that is still raging tonight. Metaphor

The conspirators told Antony he could speak at Caesar’s funeral, but only if he did not directly blame them for the killing. Antony agreed to those terms. Then he got up in front of thousands and thousands of Romans and gave the cleverest speech I have ever heard. He never once broke his promise. He never said the words “Brutus is a murderer.” He did not have to.

He kept calling Brutus “an honorable man.” Over and over and over. Every time he described something good and generous that Caesar had done for Rome, he ended with “but Brutus says he was ambitious, and Brutus is an honorable man.” By the fifteenth time, the word “honorable” did not sound like a compliment anymore. It sounded like the worst insult in the Latin language. Irony / Repetition as Rhetorical Device

Then Antony held up Caesar’s toga. Still bloody. Still full of stab holes. He pointed to each tear and named the senator who made it. He pointed to the wound from Brutus and called it “the most unkindest cut of all,” not because it hurt the most physically but because it came from the person Caesar loved and trusted most. That is not a wound that heals. Hyperbole

After all of that, he read Caesar’s will out loud. Caesar had left seventy five silver coins to every single citizen of Rome. He also left his personal gardens along the Tiber River to the public so that anyone, rich or poor, could walk there whenever they wanted.

The crowd could not handle it. The man they had just watched get stabbed to death had spent his last years making sure they had money and parks and food. People started sobbing. Then they started screaming. Then they started ripping apart wooden market stalls and benches to build a funeral pyre right there in the Forum. Mobs ran off looking for the conspirators. Brutus and Cassius had to sneak out of Rome in disguise that same night.

“He never directly accused a single person. He just laid out the facts and let the crowd do what the crowd does.” — One unnamed senator, speaking to The Tribune

Antony never technically broke any rules. But every single person who watched that speech walked away wanting the conspirators gone. It was like a river that looks calm and slow until you are already swept away. Simile

Rome · Politics

WHAT HAPPENS NOW? ROME HAS NO LEADER AND NOBODY HAS A PLAN

By Publius Aemilius Crispus · Political Desk

Caesar is gone and the Roman Republic is like a ship with no captain drifting toward rocks it cannot see yet. Metaphor The Senate met in emergency session this morning and talked for three hours without agreeing on anything at all.

Brutus stood up and tried to explain himself. He said it was not that he hated Caesar. He said he loved Rome more than he loved his friend. He said Caesar was becoming a king and kings are bad for the freedom of the Roman people. Some senators nodded. Most just sat there quietly and stared at the floor.

The problem everyone is slowly realizing is this: Brutus and the other conspirators did not plan what would happen after Caesar was dead. They thought that once he was gone the Republic would just snap back to normal and everyone would be relieved and grateful. They did not ask what would fill the space Caesar left behind. You cannot pull the center stone out of an arch and expect the arch to keep standing. Metaphor

A reconstruction of the Roman Senate building where leaders must now decide what comes next for a city that feels like it is falling apart.

Mark Antony is Caesar’s co-consul and right now he is the most powerful person in Rome. He already has Caesar’s personal papers, his money, and soldiers who are loyal to him personally. He is acting more like a leader with every passing hour.

Rumor also says that Caesar’s teenage great-nephew Octavius is on his way to Rome after hearing about the death. Sources say Caesar named him as his heir in his will. If that is true, things are going to get even messier very soon.

The conspirators are gone. Some are hiding, some have left Rome entirely. The men who said they were saving the Republic may have actually just started a civil war. History has a funny sense of humor about things like that. Personification The so-called Liberators wanted freedom and what they may have actually created is something nobody wants to think about too hard.

Editorial · The View of the Tribune

DID ROME JUST TRADE ONE BIG PROBLEM FOR AN EVEN BIGGER ONE?

By the Editorial Staff of The Roman Tribune

Let us be fair for a second. Brutus and the other conspirators were not completely crazy to worry about Caesar. He was named Dictator for Life. The Senate was losing its power to check what he did. These were real problems and it is not fair to pretend they were not.

But here is the thing that bothers this newspaper. Brutus himself admitted at his own speech that Caesar had not actually done anything wrong yet. He said the assassination was about what Caesar might do someday in the future. So the most respected senator in Rome organized a murder based on a guess about the future. Is that really justice, or is that just fear with a knife in its hand? Rhetorical Question

The men who called themselves Liberators loved Rome so much that they stabbed their friend to death in the middle of a Senate meeting and then ran away from Rome as fast as their sandals could carry them the very next day. That is a strange way to show love for a city. Irony

There is also this: Caesar was genuinely popular. He won wars that made Rome richer and safer. He gave grain to poor families. He built roads. He forgave his enemies instead of executing them, which was almost unheard of for a Roman general. He left his personal gardens to the public in his will. Whatever his faults, the common people of Rome loved this man. The conspirators forgot that. They forgot to ask the actual citizens of the Republic what they thought before doing what they did.

Now Rome faces its darkest hour in a very long time. The wound Caesar’s death has left in this city is like a crack in a dam. Simile The water is not gushing through yet but everyone who lives here can feel it coming. The Senate has no plan. There is no new leader. The men who claimed to love Rome more than anything are in hiding or gone. History has seen this before. Removing one powerful man without replacing him with something better is not liberation. It is just a different kind of chaos with better speeches.

This newspaper is not saying Caesar was perfect. He was not. But the question was never “is Caesar perfect?” The question was “what comes next?” And the conspirators had absolutely no answer for that at all.

Rome is a seven hundred year old city. She has survived wars and plagues and famines. She can survive this too. But she is going to need better leaders than the ones currently sneaking out of town in disguise to do it.

“A man can be stabbed twenty three times. An idea cannot be stabbed at all.” Metaphor

IN MEMORIAM

Gaius Julius Caesar
100 BCE — March 15, 44 BCE · Age 55 · Dictator of Rome

Gaius Julius Caesar, the greatest general Rome has probably ever had, was murdered in the Senate yesterday by a group of senators who said they were doing it for the Republic. He was fifty five years old.

Caesar was born into a noble family but he spent most of his life fighting for ordinary people. He conquered Gaul, crossed the Rubicon River, won a civil war, and was named Dictator for Life. He gave grain to the poor, built roads, and pardoned enemies instead of killing them. He was not a perfect man but he was a man who actually cared about people outside of the Senate.

A funeral pyre was built for him right in the Forum by the crowd itself, which tells you everything you need to know. He is survived by his wife Calpurnia and his great-nephew Octavius, who is on his way to Rome now. The hole his death leaves in this city is as wide as the Mediterranean Sea. Hyperbole

Cinna the Poet
Circa 90 BCE — March 15, 44 BCE · Age approx. 46 · Poet

Gaius Helvius Cinna, poet of Rome, was killed yesterday afternoon in the streets by an angry mob. He had nothing at all to do with Caesar’s death. He just had the same name as one of the conspirators and was in the wrong place at the wrong time.

The mob stopped him near the Forum and demanded he tell them who he was. He told them the truth. He was Cinna the Poet, not Cinna the Senator. They did not care, or at least some of them did not. Witnesses say someone shouted “Tear him for his bad verses!” which suggests that his poetry career had also made him some enemies along the way. A man who survived every bad review did not survive a case of mistaken identity. Dark Irony

He is survived by his family and a collection of poems that critics mostly found unimpressive.

Portia
Circa 70 BCE — 44 BCE · Age approx. 26 · Wife of Brutus

Portia, wife of Marcus Brutus and daughter of the great senator Cato the Younger, died this week in an act of terrible courage. When she heard that Brutus had fled Rome and the cause was lost, she took her own life by swallowing hot coals.

Portia was one of the bravest women in Rome. When she suspected Brutus was hiding a huge secret from her, she stabbed herself in the thigh to prove she could handle pain and keep quiet about it. It worked. Brutus told her everything about the plot. She kept that secret inside her like a stone tied around her heart. Metaphor

She was brave like her father Cato, who also chose death rather than give up what he believed in. She is survived by her husband Brutus, wherever he is hiding tonight. Allusion

Human Interest

A SOLDIER’S MOTHER SPEAKS: “MY SON FOUGHT FOR CAESAR AND I AM NOT SORRY”

By Flavia Servilius · Human Interest Desk

Livia Cornelius is sixty two years old. She lives in a small third floor apartment on the Aventine Hill with two fat cats and a shelf full of clay pots her late husband made before he got sick. She has flour on her hands most mornings and a loud laugh that you can hear from the street below. She does not look like someone with strong opinions about Roman politics. But ask her about Julius Caesar and you better have time to sit down.

Her son Marcus served under Caesar during the wars in Gaul. He came back with a small scar on his left arm, a limp that only shows up when it rains, and a bronze bracelet that Caesar personally gave to the soldiers after one of the hardest battles of the whole campaign. Livia keeps that bracelet on the kitchen table. She moves it out of the way when she is cooking and then moves it right back.

“My son ate because of Caesar,” she told The Tribune this morning while stirring barley porridge. “Before Caesar made sure soldiers actually got paid on time and the grain got distributed fairly, we went hungry some months. After that? Never once. Not a single time.”

When we told her about the assassination, she sat down on her wooden stool and was very quiet for what felt like a long time. Then she looked up and said something this reporter is not going to forget. “The senators killed him because they were scared of what he might do someday. But I know what he actually did. For my family. For my son. You tell me which one counts more.”

She is not alone in thinking this way. All across the lower districts of Rome the feeling is the same. Caesar was their champion and he is gone now. The question of who will speak for ordinary people like Livia hangs over the city tonight like smoke that will not clear. Simile She picked up the bracelet as we left. She did not say goodbye. She just held it.

THE BREAD BAKER OF THE SUBURA SAYS CAESAR’S GRAIN PROGRAM LITERALLY SAVED HIS SHOP

By Flavia Servilius · Human Interest Desk

Titus Varius has been baking bread in the same small shop near the Subura district for twenty years. He wakes up before the sun every single morning. He goes to bed after everyone else. He is not rich, he is not famous, and he has never once been invited to a dinner party at anyone’s villa. He is just a man with flour on his apron and a stone oven that never quite gets warm enough in the winter months.

But when Julius Caesar introduced the grain dole years ago to make sure poor Romans could actually afford food, Titus says his entire business changed overnight. “Before that, maybe half my customers could buy bread every single day. After the grain dole? Almost all of them could. I hired two more people. I fixed my roof. I stopped worrying every single morning about whether anyone was going to walk through that door.”

Titus heard the news of the assassination yesterday morning while he was pulling a batch of loaves out of the oven. He says he just stood there holding the long wooden paddle and let the whole batch burn. Just stood there and stared at nothing for he does not know how long.

“I know senators think about big important things like laws and armies and whatever,” he said, wiping his hands on his apron. “I think about bread. I think about whether the people in my neighborhood can eat today. Caesar thought about that too. That is why people like me are very sad right now.”

Grain prices in the markets have already gone up in the last few hours as merchants start to sense trouble coming. Titus says if things keep going the way they are going, he may have to raise his own prices for the first time in ten years. For the families of the Subura who budget every single coin, that would hurt just as much as anything happening up in the Senate. Maybe more. Allusion to the gap between the powerful and the ordinary
